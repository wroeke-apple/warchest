Dossier Nº 1

Michigan — the open seat that prices the 2024 playbook

The situation

Gary Peters is retiring. In 2024, Elissa Slotkin held this state by 0.3 points while running 1.7 ahead of the top of the ticket — proof the seat is winnable with discipline, and a warning about the margin for error. Three Democrats now sit within $1.3M of each other; the Republican side has consolidated early behind Mike Rogers, who lost to Slotkin by 0.3 and returns with $4.3M banked.

The case

A purple-state open seat where the primary — not the general — is where a marginal dollar buys the most: it selects the posture (institutional, insurgent, or movement) that meets a known Republican quantity in November.

The risk

A three-way primary burning at 57–67% while Rogers banks. If the nominee emerges in August cash-poor against a consolidated opponent, 2024's 0.3-point margin becomes the optimistic case.

What we are watching

Q2 filings (Jul 15): whether any candidate breaks from the pack; first television reservations; whether El-Sayed's small-dollar machine (Middle Seat, $1.4M so far) converts to a cash edge or consumes it; any 2024-set firms joining rosters — each hire is a strategy signal.

II.

Analyst's Note

Written from the filings above. Every figure is computed in this bulletin; nothing is sourced from rumor.

The map first. Democrats are defending Georgia and Michigan and chasing open seats in Kentucky, North Carolina, Iowa, Minnesota, New Hampshire, and Illinois; five incumbent-party retirements — McConnell, Peters, Smith, Shaheen, Durbin — plus the open Tillis and Ernst seats make this the most open Senate map in a decade. Across the thirteen races tracked in this bulletin, declared candidates have already raised $482.7 million, and the vendor rosters that will spend it are forming now, sixteen months before the general.

Texas is running a money experiment on itself. James Talarico has raised $40.3M — more than every Republican in the race combined ($17.6M) — but 42 percent of his spending to date ($12.7M) is fundraising cost, including $10.8M to a single digital-fundraising firm. That is the machine that built the number; the question a donor should ask is what the net buys in a state Democrats lost by 8.5 points last cycle. Jasmine Crockett has raised $11.1M and holds $0.6M. Colin Allred — $80M+ raised across his 2024 run — is back with $7.6M raised and $12,000 cash on hand.

A burn alert in Florida. Josh Weil's committee shows $15.9M raised and $0.0M cash, with 46 cents of every raised dollar going to fundraising vendors — a structure in which donations chiefly purchase more donations. Alexander Vindman shows the opposite shape: $8.2M raised, 21 percent burn, $6.4M banked. Same state, same party, radically different capital efficiency — and no donor-side instrument but this one displays the difference.

Maine is a machine-versus-machine test. Graham Platner has out-raised Governor Mills nearly three to one ($16.3M against $5.8M) while burning 87 cents of each dollar, much of it through digital fundraising and early television. Susan Collins sits on $9.7M at a 42 percent burn. The primary will decide whether small-dollar velocity or institutional discipline meets her in October.

Michigan is a photo finish — read the rosters, not the toplines. Stevens ($8.9M), McMorrow ($8.6M), and El-Sayed ($7.7M) sit within $1.3M of one another. The hires say more: El-Sayed's email program runs through the firm that served Casey, Warren, and Kunce in 2024, while the Rogers operation opposite is already at $7.7M. This is the seat where the 2024 Slotkin playbook — +1.7 against the top of the ticket — is stress-tested without Slotkin.

Ohio: the rematch is being run differently. Sherrod Brown's 2026 committee has raised $26.0M at a 34 percent burn with $17.0M banked — against 2024, when his campaign spent $97.5M, two-thirds of it on media, and lost by 3.6 while running 7.6 ahead of the ticket. Husted holds $8.2M. The discipline is new; whether it survives ad season is what the Q3 filing will tell us.

Kentucky, an uncomfortable signal. Amy McGrath — whose 2020 race absorbed more than $90M for a nineteen-point loss — has filed for the open McConnell seat: $2.2M raised, 96 percent burn to date. The Republican primary is the live contest (Barr $8.4M, Morris $8.6M, Cameron $2.2M). Donors will be solicited hard here; the 2020 file is the base rate.

On the watch list: Roy Cooper, the cycle's cash position leader ($18.5M banked, 31 percent burn); Ossoff's $32.5M fortress; Pappas consolidating New Hampshire while Sununu and Brown divide the other lane. The second-quarter filings land July 15. Every burn rate in this bulletin will be re-scored within forty-eight hours.

VII.

Method

2026 live data: FEC bulk candidate summaries and operating expenditures, 2025–26 cycle, pulled July 6, 2026. Coverage varies by filer (quarterly campaigns report through March 31; monthly filers later — dates shown per candidate). Candidates filtered to the 2026 ballot and $250K+ raised.

Burn is disbursements over receipts, cycle to date; high early burn is a question, not a verdict — which is why the rosters show whom the money went to. Fundraising cost is payments classified to fundraising, processing, and acquisition roles, over receipts.

2024 layer: the principal committees of all thirty-one Democratic Senate nominees; certified results; ±Ticket is the Senate margin less the Harris margin in the same state. Vendor records appear only at n≥3, and gross is not fee income for placement roles. Maine 2024 is an outlier by construction (Angus King). Every number derives from public filings; the production system links each figure to its filing image.

Your Statement collects nothing. You select campaigns; the bulletin aggregates their public spending.
